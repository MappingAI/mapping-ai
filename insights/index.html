Structure of the AI Governance Ecosystem

We mapped — entities in U.S. AI policy. Rather than claiming what percentage believes what, we focus on structure: how the ecosystem is organized, who connects to whom, and where the gaps lie.

This page presents structural analyses of the Mapping AI database. Our guiding principle: avoid selection bias by focusing on in-group comparisons rather than extrapolating to the broader population. We're interested in relational patterns — who clusters with whom, what beliefs travel together, which communities are bridged or siloed.

Section 1

The Belief Space

We track three belief dimensions for people and organizations: regulatory stance (accelerate → precautionary), AGI timeline (already here → 25+ years), and AI risk level (overstated → existential). These form a three-dimensional "belief space" where entities can be positioned.

Belief axis distributions (people + orgs with data)
Horizontal bars show count at each level. Entities without data excluded.

The distributions reveal which positions are crowded and which are sparse. A heavily populated region suggests mainstream consensus; empty regions may indicate underrepresented perspectives or simply positions few people hold.

Bridges and silos

Some entities connect otherwise disconnected communities — they're bridges. Others exist in tightly-knit clusters with few outside ties — they're siloed. Identifying bridges helps understand how ideas cross community boundaries.

Most connected entities by degree
Degree = number of direct relationships. Higher degree = more central to the network.
Open question

Do frontier labs → think tanks → government represent a reliable idea-flow pipeline, or is the network more decentralized? This requires directional edge analysis we haven't yet implemented.
