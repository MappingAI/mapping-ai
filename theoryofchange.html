Project 2029 — Theory of Change

The Endless Frontier, Revisited

Working Draft · March 2026
The problem

AI is a step-function change that demands a fundamental renegotiation between government, industry, and the public. In short, a new technology-society compact. Neither party currently has a coherent position on what that renegotiation should look like. The conversation is stuck in old paradigms – the US-China race, accelerationism vs. safety, regulate vs. accelerate – none of which answer the foundational question: on what terms is the value of American technological innovation delivered?

The historical record offers a clear model: when a step-function technology arrives, societies that build new institutions to match the moment distribute its benefits broadly. Electrification is the proof of concept. By 1935, 90% of urban homes had power and 10% of rural ones did, forty years after the technology existed. FDR didn't wait for markets or subsidize private utilities; he built new institutional forms, the Rural Electrification Administration and cooperative financing structures that communities owned themselves. By 1950, the gap had closed. AI has arrived faster than electricity ever did, and unlike electricity it carries risks that demand governance not just distribution. History suggests we have a narrow window to build the right institutions before private capture becomes the structural default. That window is now.

AI's progression is unlike that of prior technologies. A handful of firms now control not just a market or an infrastructure, but the frontier of knowledge production itself, built atop decades of publicly funded research, open-source infrastructure, human-generated data, and combined human ingenuity. Yet these firms currently have no obligation to return value to the public. There is no compact governing this relationship, and the shared inheritance has suffered as a result.

The existing landscape

Each camp has a diagnosis. None has a compact. This is the vacuum we are filling.

The right
Win the race
Deregulate, accelerate, dominate. The US-China frame defines everything. Distribution is an afterthought; workforce training is the concession to labor. Preempting state regulation is the active political agenda right now.
↗ America's AI Action Plan ↗ Dean Ball, "How I Approach AI Policy"
Democratic establishment
Safety and transition
Privacy, civil rights, job protections, responsible deployment. Senator Kelly's AI Horizon Fund would tax AI gains to fund workforce retraining. Sanders and Khanna have centered labor's voice in how AI is deployed. Proposals have not yet cohered into a structural compact.
↗ Kelly, AI for America ↗ Khanna, "A New Tech Social Contract" ↗ Karen Hao, Empire of AI ↗ Kagan-Kans, "The Left is Missing Out on AI"
Safety / alignment — short timelines camp
Race to safety through speed
AGI is imminent; the priority is ensuring the right actors win. Concentrated control in "responsible" Western labs is preferable to fragmentation. A smaller but vocal camp, Yudkowsky and Soares being prominent voices, argues that alignment is unsolved and building superintelligence before it is solved risks catastrophic, potentially irreversible outcomes.
↗ Aschenbrenner, "Situational Awareness" ↗ AI 2027 (Kokotajlo et al.) ↗ Dario Amodei, "Machines of Loving Grace" ↗ Yudkowsky & Soares, If Anyone Builds It, Everyone Dies
Safety / governance — institutional camp
Govern the technology, not the race
Focused on near-term harms, accountability, auditing, and institutional design. Closer to our frame — Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy is the most operationalized safety-governance bridge — but still largely defensive and decoupled from distribution and labor. The "normal technology" counter argues AI will diffuse slowly, giving institutions time to respond. The subset working on power concentration is a natural coalition partner.
↗ Anthropic Responsible Scaling Policy ↗ Narayanan & Kapoor, "AI as Normal Technology"
Our operating principle

Commonwealth: AI built for the public. Systemic by design rather than redistributive downstream, built into how innovation is financed, the infrastructure we construct, the knowledge we maintain and the benefits we distribute. This framing unifies labor, safety, national security, and institutional design under the one question no camp is currently answering: who captures value from American innovation, and on what terms? That is the question no camp is currently answering and this project is built around.

The mechanisms
01 — Institutional construction
Government should be a maker, not only a regulator or a customer. The REA not only regulated private utilities into serving rural America, it built a new institutional form that private utilities never would. A new compact for AI requires the same constructive ambition: an AI Accounting Office that continuously measures how capabilities are developing and how broadly benefits are flowing; a Digital REA that builds public compute and open AI infrastructure alongside private offerings; and a regulatory body with an affirmative mandate to build, not just constrain.
02 — Return architecture
Public investment should generate public return by structure, not by taxation after concentration has already occurred. The problem with redistribution as a governance strategy is that it operates on outcomes while leaving intact the architecture of who captures value in the first place. A new compact intervenes at the source. That means cooperative ownership structures where the people receiving a service own the infrastructure, shared resources treated as public property from the start, financing instruments that don't require monopolistic scale to be worthwhile. These are early directions in a design space still being built out. The principle is fixed even where the instruments are not: return by design.
03 — Adaptive governance
The world is shifting faster than any legislative cycle can track. Sunset clauses and mandatory review periods start to address this, but they are scheduled flexibility rather than genuine adaptability. Adaptive governance operates at three layers. The first is sensing: a continuous, real-time picture of what the technology is doing and who it is affecting, independent of the entities being measured. The second is response: pre-committed mechanisms triggered by observable conditions rather than political calendars. The third is recalibration: a legitimate process for updating the thresholds and mechanisms themselves as understanding evolves, rather than waiting for legislative inertia to catch up. The closest existing models are in deliberative democracy, like Taiwan's Alignment Assemblies. A broader international survey of adaptive governance experiments is part of this project's ongoing research agenda.
04 — Coalitions
Safety researchers, governance scholars, labor advocates, national security strategists, civil rights organizations, and industry leaders are each developing answers to related questions, in different languages, with different threat models. Few are building across them. We work with anyone who agrees that AI's capabilities should serve human flourishing. The goal is a governing agenda, specific, staffed, and deployable on day one. Operationally, we aim for Project 2025 seriousness oriented toward building public capacity rather than dismantling it.
05 — Pluralism
The current trajectory of AI development tends toward convergence: a small number of large general-purpose foundation models, built on overlapping data and shared architectures, concentrated in the same geographies and capital structures. Monocultures lack resilience. When one architecture has a fundamental blind spot, models trained on it tend to share this vulnerability. Diversity of approaches, ownership structures, purposes, and scales is a property of healthy technological ecosystems. Pluralism as a governance mechanism could mean public financing for smaller purpose-specific models serving communities the market won't reach, open-source infrastructure maintained as a public good, institutional diversity in who conducts AI research, hardware speciation that avoids single points of failure, and alignment practices that reflect a range of human values.
