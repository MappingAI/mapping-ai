Mapping AI

Mapping the U.S. AI Policy Landscape

What

We are building a comprehensive map of the organizations, individuals, and coalitions currently shaping or with the potential to influence AI policy in the United States leading up to 2028. The goal is to produce a structured, shareable, and dynamic resource that identifies who is working on what, where the gaps are, and where coalitions might form across ideological lines.

Why Now

AI policy in the U.S. is being shaped rapidly, but the landscape is fragmented. Safety researchers, frontier labs, think tanks, state legislators, labor organizers, academics, and civil society all have stakes in AI governance, yet there is no shared, public map (that we know of) of who is doing what. The 2026 midterms and 2028 presidential cycle will produce technology policy platforms on both sides, and the intellectual groundwork is being laid now. A clear map of the landscape, worldviews, and competing theories of change is a prerequisite for any serious effort to contribute.

The Gap

Two recent pieces illustrate what exists and what is missing. Michel Justen's "A Guide to the AI Tribes" (Jan 2026) provides a useful taxonomy of the ideological camps shaping AI discourse. Dan Kagan-Kans' "The Left is Missing Out on AI" (Feb 2026) makes a compelling case that left and progressive institutions have largely refused to engage seriously with AI.

Yet we have not seen anything that maps the full potential of the policy landscape: the frontier labs and safety orgs, but also the investors, think tanks, state legislators, labor organizers, academics, creative guilds, and political operatives whose work will determine what AI policy actually looks like. We aim to fill that gap.

Vision

If this mapping effort is successful, we hope it can build toward a coalition around creating a technology policy platform for 2028 that is transformative in the way Vannevar Bush's Endless Frontier was for the postwar era: not just regulating emerging technology, but building the institutions and explicating the science-society compact to ensure its benefits are broadly shared and its risks are pragmatically mitigated.
