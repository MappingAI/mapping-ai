# **Mapping the U.S. AI Policy Landscape**

A collaborative stakeholder mapping project  ·  March 2026

**WHAT**

We are building a comprehensive map of the organizations, individuals, and coalitions currently shaping or with the potential to influence AI policy in the United States leading up to 2028\. The goal is to produce a structured, shareable, and dynamic resource that identifies who is working on what, where the gaps are, and where coalitions might form across ideological lines.

**WHY NOW**

AI policy in the U.S. is being shaped rapidly, but the landscape is fragmented. Safety researchers, frontier labs, think tanks, state legislators, labor organizers, academics, and civil society all have stakes in AI governance, yet there is no shared, public map (that we know of) of who is doing what. The 2026 midterms and 2028 presidential cycle will produce technology policy platforms on both sides, and the intellectual groundwork is being laid now. A clear map of the landscape, worldviews, and competing theories of change is a prerequisite for any serious effort to contribute.

**THE GAP**

Two recent pieces illustrate what exists and what is missing. Michel Justen’s [“A Guide to the AI Tribes”](https://substack.com/home/post/p-185759007) (Jan 2026\) provides a useful taxonomy of the ideological camps shaping AI discourse: AI safety, ethics and bias, e/acc, the tech right, and the MAGA base. Dan Kagan-Kans’ [“The Left is Missing Out on AI”](https://www.transformernews.ai/p/the-left-is-missing-out-on-ai-sanders-doctorow-bender-bores) (Feb 2026\) makes a compelling case that left and progressive institutions have largely refused to engage seriously with AI, ceding debate about a threat (and opportunity) to the right. As Joshua Achiam of OpenAI put it: when there is a major problem that will be top of mind for everyone in a decade, whoever engages first sets the terms of discussion.

Yet we have not seen anything that maps the *full* potential of the policy landscape: the frontier labs and safety orgs, but also the investors, think tanks, state legislators, labor organizers, academics, creative guilds, and political operatives whose work and theories of change will determine what AI policy actually looks like. The AI Tribes taxonomy stops at ideology; the [aisafety.com](https://www.aisafety.com/map) map covers the safety ecosystem but not the broader policy, labor, and political actors. We aim to fill that gap.

If this mapping effort is successful, we hope it can build toward a coalition around creating a technology policy platform for 2028 that is transformative in the way Vannevar Bush’s *Endless Frontier* was for the postwar era: not just regulating emerging technology, but building the institutions and explicating the science-society compact to ensure its benefits are broadly shared and its risks are pragmatically mitigated.

**POSSIBLE OUTPUTS**

The mapping may produce the following example types of artifacts, and media/communications to share it widely:

**1\. A structured stakeholder database (Airtable)** tracking organizations, individuals, policy efforts, and influential thought pieces. Linked tables allow mapping people to organizations, tracking contact networks and connector scores, and identifying which actors influence funding, policy, research, or public discourse. (See screenshot mock-up below.)

**2\. Visual landscape maps** that place stakeholders along key axes of disagreement and influence. The most revealing axes are likely: (a) capability timeline belief (normal technology vs. transformative/x-risk), (b) regulatory stance (pro-safety vs. pro-innovation, more vs. less regulation, institution-building vs. tax-and-incentivize), and (c) present harms vs. future risk focus. These could correspond to the axes similar to those in the AI Tribes taxonomy and may surface where surprising coalitions could exist.
