Mapping Party

Help us map the people, organizations, and ideas shaping U.S. AI policy

We've been building a stakeholder map of the American AI policy landscape and it's almost ready for a public launch. Pick a stream below and get started.

What is Mapping AI?

Mapping AI is an interactive map of the U.S. AI policy landscape. It tracks the people, organizations, and resources involved in how AI gets governed in America: who they are, what they believe about regulation, and how they connect to each other. Policymakers, lab execs, researchers, civil society groups, investors, journalists.

The database currently has about 709 people, 734 organizations, and 161 resources, linked by 2,228 relationships. About 1,020 of those are publicly visible on the map. The rest are pending review or don't have enough info to publish yet.

The tech stack is simple: static HTML pages, D3.js for the visualization, PostgreSQL on AWS, and a few Lambda functions for form submissions and search. People submit data through a form on the site, it goes into a review queue, and approved entries show up on the map.

Heads up: we're still in beta. We soft-launched to a small group about a week ago and there's a lot to improve.

Data flow

Data moves through the system in two ways:

  PATH 1: How the map loads (fast, no database call)
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Push to main
    -> GitHub Actions: build TipTap, run export-map-data.js (queries DB)
    -> Uploads HTML + CSS + JS + map-data.json to S3
    -> Invalidates CloudFront cache
    -> Users load map.html, browser fetches map-data.json from CDN
    -> That's it. The map never talks to the database directly.

  PATH 2: How new data gets in (form submissions)
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────
  User fills out contribute form
    -> POST /submit -> Lambda -> inserts into submission table (status: pending)
    -> Claude Haiku reviews it in the background (rates quality 1-5)
  Admin approves the submission
    -> DB trigger auto-creates entity row, recalculates belief scores
    -> Lambda regenerates map-data.json, uploads to S3, invalidates CDN
    -> Map reflects the change within about a minute

The map is just a static JSON file on a CDN. No real-time database queries, no server rendering. It loads fast everywhere because D3 reads the JSON and draws the graph client-side.

Stream 1: Bug Hunting

Best for: anyone with a browser and some patience

This is where we need the most help. The site has a lot of interactive components that need real user testing - things that work fine for the developer who built them but break in unexpected ways when someone else tries them. Open the site, click around, try weird inputs, resize your window, test on your phone. The examples below are just starting points - the best bugs are often found by doing things we didn't think to list. If something looks off or doesn't work, write it up.

Where to look

The map is where most of the complexity is. Some starting points, but don't limit yourself to these:

  • Search for people and organizations
  • Click nodes to see their detail panel
  • Toggle between Network and Plot views
  • Try the filter chips
  • Zoom in and out
  • Collapse the controls sidebar and re-expand it
  • Open the contribute panel from the map page and see if it plays nicely with everything else

The contribute form has three tabs (Person, Organization, Resource), each with custom dropdowns, tag inputs, and a rich text editor. Some starting points:

  • Fill out a form completely and submit it
  • Try the org search
  • Create a new org from the inline panel that slides in
  • @mention someone in the notes field
  • Try location search
  • See what happens with empty required fields

You'll have a separate .env file with credentials to check the admin panel and see how your submissions are being received in the database.

The homepage and about page are simpler but worth a quick check too, especially on mobile.

How to report bugs

Log bugs in this shared doc using this format:

Page: [which page]
What I did: [steps to reproduce]
What I expected: [what should have happened]
What happened: [what actually happened]
Browser/device: [Chrome on Mac, Safari on iPhone, etc.]
Screenshot: [paste one if you can]

Functional stuff takes priority over cosmetic stuff. A dropdown that won't close matters more than a font that looks slightly off. But note both if you see them.

Stream 2: Data Enrichment & Seeding

Best for: people who follow AI policy or like doing research

The map is only as useful as its data. Most entities now have notes, but many are thin, unsourced, or out of date. Your job today is to add new entities we're missing and flesh out ones that are already there.

Start by exploring the current data. Browse the map to see what's there. You also have access to the staging database (via the .env file) and can query it directly or look at the exported map-data.json. This will help you identify gaps and avoid duplicating work.

What to add

Think about who's missing. Use the search bar on the map to make sure the person or org isn't already in there. Some areas that are particularly sparse right now:

  • State-level policymakers working on AI (governors, AGs, state legislators)
  • Congressional staffers and committee staff who actually draft AI bills
  • AI companies beyond the big frontier labs (defense tech, enterprise AI, infrastructure providers)
  • Civil society groups, labor unions, advocacy organizations
  • International organizations with real U.S. influence (OECD AI Policy Observatory, UN AI Advisory Body, etc.)
  • Journalists and media figures who cover AI policy
  • Lawyers and legal scholars working on AI regulation
  • Industry trade associations and lobbying groups
  • Recent key resources: executive orders, major reports, papers, influential essays or books from the last year or so

What to enrich

Beyond adding new entities, you can also improve existing ones:

  • Add missing edges (relationships). The map is a network - connections between entities matter as much as the entities themselves. Edge types include employment, advisory roles, board memberships, funding, collaborations, founding relationships, mentorship, critics/supporters, and more - see workshop/DATABASE-ORIENTATION.md for the full list. About 30% of current edges lack supporting evidence, so adding sources helps too.
  • Fill in belief fields. Many entities are missing regulatory stance, AGI timeline beliefs, or AI risk assessments. If you can find evidence (quotes, testimony, published positions), add it.
  • Expand thin notes. Some entries have just a sentence or two. Add context about why someone matters to AI policy, what they've done, who they work with.
  • Add sources. Notes without sources are harder to verify. If you can find primary sources for claims, add them to the notes_sources field.

How to submit

There are two options:

Option 1: Direct to staging database (recommended for workshop)

You'll receive a .env file with credentials for the staging database, which lets you read and write directly via SQL. This is a complete copy of production data that you can freely modify without affecting the live site. To see your changes on the map:

npm install
source .env                       # load database credentials
node scripts/export-map-data.js   # exports map-data.json from staging DB
npx serve .                       # runs local server on localhost:3000

After making changes to the database, re-run export-map-data.js and refresh your browser to see the updated map. See workshop/DATABASE-ORIENTATION.md for the full schema reference, example queries, and quality guidelines.

Option 2: Live contribute form

Go directly to mapping-ai.org/contribute and submit there. Entries go into the production database and will appear on the live map after admin approval.

A few things worth knowing about the form:

The relationship pills at the top of each form actually matter for how data gets weighted. "I am this person" means self-reported info (weighted highest). "I can connect you" means you know them personally. "Someone I know of" means you're working from public sources.

The notes field is the most valuable part of any submission. Write 2-4 sentences about why this person or organization matters to U.S. AI policy. Don't just paste their Wikipedia bio. What have they done, said, or funded that shapes how AI gets built or governed? Use the @mention feature to link to other entities already in the database.

For the belief fields (regulatory stance, AGI timeline, AI risk level), you can fill these in if you have evidence for them, such as a direct quote, published position paper, congressional testimony, or other online statements that you can reference. Otherwise leave them blank.

Here's roughly what a good person entry looks like:

Name: Chuck Schumer
Title: U.S. Senator, NY; Senate Majority Leader (2021-2025), Senate Minority Leader (2017-2021, 2025-present)
Category: Policymaker
Primary Org: United States Senate
Other Orgs: Bipartisan Senate AI Working Group (Co-founder/Leader), Senate Finance Committee, Senate Rules Committee
Location: New York, NY
Twitter: @SenSchumer
Notes: Chuck Schumer is a Democratic U.S. Senator from New York who has served since 1999 and is currently Senate Majority Leader. He led a bipartisan working group that released a 31-page AI policy roadmap in May 2024, recommending at least $32 billion in government spending to accelerate AI research and development. The roadmap was developed after months of AI Insight Forums with tech companies, civil rights leaders, and other stakeholders. Schumer has described regulating artificial intelligence as "a challenge for Congress unlike any other" and has made election protection from AI interference a high priority.
Regulatory stance: Moderate
Stance detail: Advocates for 'Safe Innovation Framework' emphasizing innovation as 'North Star' while establishing necessary guardrails. Supports targeted regulations like federal AI procurement guidelines, election deepfake protections, and the DEFIANCE Act against non-consensual AI pornography.
AGI timeline: 5-10 years
AI risk level: Serious
Threat models: Labor displacement, Power concentration, Democratic erosion, Misinformation

Quality over quantity

Five well-researched submissions with sourced notes are worth more than twenty stubs with just a name and title. Everything goes through admin review before it shows up on the map, so don't worry about mistakes, but do your homework.

Stream 3: Data Quality & Verification

Best for: skeptics, fact-checkers, people who notice when something seems off

The database was built through a combination of manual research, web scraping, and AI-assisted enrichment, so it contains unverifiable claims, stale information, and some outright fabrications. Your job is to find and flag these.

Start by exploring the data. Browse the map and click on nodes to read their detail panels. You also have access to the staging database (via the .env file) where you can run queries to find patterns of issues, or look at the exported map-data.json directly.

Known issues to look for

  • Hallucinated facts. The AI-generated notes sometimes include claims that sound very specific and plausible but are just wrong. Suspiciously precise founding dates, round dollar amounts, inflated competition results, awards that don't exist. If something sounds weirdly specific, it's worth a quick check.
  • Stale information. Some entries list people at organizations they've since left. Government officials who changed roles after the 2025 transition come up a lot.
  • Wrong org affiliations. Primary org set to a completely different entity with a similar-sounding name. These are especially bad because they create false connections on the map.
  • Edges missing evidence. About 30% of relationships have no supporting evidence. If a relationship seems questionable, check whether there's evidence for it.
  • Thin or missing context. Notes that are technically accurate but don't explain why someone matters to AI policy. "John Smith is the CEO of Acme Corp" is less useful than explaining what Acme Corp does and why it's relevant.

How to fix issues

When you find something wrong, fix it directly:

  • Option 1: Staging database. Use the .env file credentials to connect to the staging DB and run UPDATE queries. See workshop/DATABASE-ORIENTATION.md for examples.
  • Option 2: Contribute form. Go to mapping-ai.org/contribute and submit a correction. This goes into the production review queue.

Start with well-known figures whose facts you can check from memory. Then move to less-familiar entities where you'll need to do some quick googling.

Stream 4: New Features

Best for: developers who want to write code

It's a static site with a D3.js map, AWS Lambda functions for the API, and a PostgreSQL database. If you can write HTML, CSS, JavaScript, or Node.js, you can contribute.

Getting set up

Clone the repo and start a local server:

git clone https://github.com/MappingAI/mapping-ai.git
cd mapping-ai
npm install
npx serve .    # runs on localhost:3000

This serves static files locally. API calls (form submissions, search) go directly to the production Lambda endpoints, so you don't need AWS credentials or a local database to work on frontend stuff.

If you want to build the TipTap rich text editor bundle (only needed if you're touching the notes editor):

npm run build:tiptap

If you're working with the staging database (for data-related features), you'll need the .env file - see workshop/DATABASE-ORIENTATION.md.

Project structure

The files you'll probably care about:

FileWhat it does
map.htmlThe D3.js map. All the visualization logic lives inline in one large script block. Loads map-data.json, a static file generated from the database.
contribute.htmlThe submission forms. Custom dropdowns, tag inputs, org search, location search, TipTap rich text editor, autosave to localStorage. It's big.
admin.htmlAdmin panel: review queue, entity editing, dashboard stats.
api/*.jsLambda functions. submit.js handles form submissions, search.js does full-text search, admin.js handles the review queue and entity CRUD, upload.js handles image uploads, export-map.js generates the map JSON.
template.yamlAWS SAM template that defines all the infrastructure.

Feature ideas

Roughly ordered by priority. Pick whatever interests you, or pitch something we haven't thought of.

  • Better search. The current search is basic and not very useful. Improving search relevance, adding filters, and making autocomplete smarter would have high impact.
  • Admin panel improvements. Right now we mostly use backend SQL to approve entities because admin.html is limited. Better bulk actions, filtering, and entity editing UI would speed up the review process significantly.
  • Shareable entity links. Clicking a node on the map doesn't update the URL, so you can't link someone to a specific entity. Having map.html?entity=123 or map.html?name=OpenAI auto-select and zoom to that node on load would be really useful.
  • Timeline view. The database has edge start/end dates (when someone joined or left an org, when a relationship began). A timeline slider or playback feature showing how the network evolved over time would be great.
  • Improved Plot view. The Plot view (scatter/beeswarm) works but is bare. Better axis labels, a legend, and the ability to color by a different dimension than the axes would all help.
  • Entity edit history. The database tracks submissions but there's no UI for seeing what changed over time for a given entity. Would be valuable for data quality work.
  • Export/embed. Let people export a filtered view of the map as PNG or SVG, or generate an embed snippet for a blog post or report.

You'll receive a .env file with credentials for the staging database (read/write) and production database (read-only) so you can test features that interact with data.

Before starting on something, check the Discord to see what others are working on and post what you're tackling. When you're done, open a pull request against main.

Conventions

Commit messages use conventional prefixes: feat:, fix:, refactor:, docs:. There are no tests (we know). CSS is inline per page, not shared. The map loads map-data.json which uses different field names than the database columns (there's a mapping layer in api/export-map.js that handles the translation). The CLAUDE.md file in the repo root has full documentation of the schema, field mappings, and architecture if you want the deep dive.

Stream 5: Outreach, Policy, Big Picture

Best for: people who think about audiences, distribution, and positioning

The map works. The question is who should be using it and why they'd come back. This stream is about figuring out where Mapping AI fits in the AI policy conversation and how to get it in front of the right people.

Questions to discuss

Who is this for? Journalists covering AI? Hill staffers researching a bill? Academics studying the policy landscape? Advocacy groups planning campaigns? AI company employees trying to understand the regulatory environment? The answer shapes what data we prioritize and how we describe the project.

What's the pitch? When you tell someone about Mapping AI in one sentence, what do you say? We've been describing it as "an interactive map of the U.S. AI policy ecosystem" but that's abstract. What would make someone click the link?

Where do we distribute? Think specific channels. Which Substacks, podcasts, Slack communities, Twitter/Bluesky accounts, university departments, think tank mailing lists, or Hill offices should know about this? Make a concrete list with links where possible.

What partnerships make sense? Are there organizations doing adjacent work who might want to share data, co-brand something, or just link to the map? Groups like the AI Policy Institute, Federation of American Scientists, university AI policy programs, journalism outlets covering AI.

What should we write about? We're planning to publish research insights from the database (see Stream 6). What angles would actually get attention? What would be useful to the policy community versus what's just content for the sake of content?

Deliverable

By the end of the session, try to write up a short strategy doc: target audiences ranked by priority, 10-20 specific distribution channels with links, a draft one-line pitch, and any partnership leads you identified. A Google Doc is fine.

Stream 6: Data Viz & Research Insights

Best for: data people, researchers, anyone who likes poking around datasets

The database has data on regulatory stances, AGI timeline beliefs, organizational networks, funding relationships, geographic clustering, and nobody has done much analysis on any of it yet. Your job is to pull out findings and sketch visualizations or rough blog post drafts we could publish.

Getting the data

The public map data is a single JSON file:

https://mapping-ai.org/map-data.json

This has all approved entities with fields already mapped to their frontend names. You can fetch it and work with it in whatever you prefer: Python (pandas, matplotlib, seaborn), R, Observable notebooks, Excel.

We also have the full database export (all 1,604 entities and 2,228 edges, including pending and internal ones) if you want it. Just ask.

Research questions worth exploring

Regulatory stance by sector. How do different types of organizations and people break down on AI regulation? You'd expect frontier labs to skew lighter-touch than think tanks, but by how much? And where do researchers land relative to executives? A stacked bar chart or alluvial diagram by category could tell a clear story here.

Network structure. Who are the most connected nodes? Are there distinct communities in the network, and if so, what defines them? Which entities bridge otherwise disconnected groups? Standard network analysis stuff (degree centrality, betweenness, community detection) would be interesting to run on this data.

AGI timeline beliefs vs. regulatory stance. Is there actually a correlation between when someone thinks AGI arrives and how much regulation they want? A scatter plot colored by entity type might show something interesting here.

Geography. Where are AI policy actors concentrated? Is it really just DC and SF, or is there more spread than people assume? A treemap or simple map of entity locations would show this quickly.

The resource landscape. What kinds of AI policy resources are in the database? Who writes them, which orgs publish the most, and how does the distribution break down by type and year? Might highlight gaps worth filling.

Funding flows. The edge data includes "funded_by" relationships. Can you trace funding networks and see whether funders cluster around particular policy positions?

Output format

Ideally, aim for something we could turn into a blog post or Substack piece. A clear finding in one sentence, a visualization that backs it up, and a couple paragraphs of context. It doesn't need to be polished. A Jupyter notebook or Observable notebook with rough charts and some annotations is plenty. We'll clean it up later.
