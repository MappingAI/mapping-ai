Mapping the U.S. AI Policy Landscape

Contribute to the Map
What

We are building a comprehensive map of the individuals and groups with the potential to shape AI policy in the United States. The goal is to produce a structured, shareable, and dynamic resource that identifies who is working on what, where the gaps are, and which coalitions might form across ideological and organizational lines.

Why Now

The landscape of AI policy in the U.S. is fragmented. Safety researchers, frontier labs, legislators, civil society, and investors all have a stake in AI governance, yet there is no public resource showing where these actors stand on critical issues, such as the technology's capabilities and the role of public policy. The 2026 midterms and 2028 presidential elections are rapidly approaching, and these groups have already laid the intellectual groundwork for candidates' technology platforms. A clear layout of the competing worldviews and theories of change is a necessary step to political coordination. This work is long overdue—those who engage first will set the terms of discussion.

The Gap

Two recent pieces illustrate what exists and what is missing. Michel Justen's "A Guide to the AI Tribes" (Jan 2026) provides a useful taxonomy of the ideological camps shaping AI discourse. Dan Kagan-Kans' "The Left is Missing Out on AI" (Feb 2026) makes a compelling case that progressive institutions have largely refused to engage seriously with AI.

Yet we have not seen anything that maps the full potential of the policy landscape: the frontier labs and safety orgs, but also the investors, think tanks, state legislators, labor organizers, academics, creative guilds, and political operatives whose work will determine what AI policy actually looks like. We aim to fill that gap.

Vision

A successful mapping effort is one that identifies and fuses a new coalition around AI governance. Ultimately, we hope this coalition creates a technology policy platform for 2028 that is as transformative for the AI era as Vannevar Bush's Endless Frontier was for the postwar period: not just regulating emerging technology, but articulating the science-society compact and building the institutions to realize that ideal. Only then can we ensure the benefits of new technology are broadly shared, and the risks pragmatically mitigated.
